iPancreas

Tools for being a better pancreas.

The Problem

Those of us with some type of diabetes (whether type 1, type 2, or monogenic) take on the task of either partially or fully simulating the functioning of a biologically normal person's pancreas. In short, we become our own pancreases. The tools we have to do this today--analog insulins, home blood glucose monitors, insulin pumps, and continuous blood glucose monitors--are much, much better than the tools that were available to persons with diabetes (PWDs) even a generation ago.

But unfortunately, the tools at our disposal for managing the data that is associated with these devices and technologies are very poor indeed. Some are not available to everyone (the software for the Dexcom continuous glucose monitor only runs on the Windows operating system); some are fiendishly difficult to operate (uploading data from an Animas/OneTouch Ping insulin pump into the Animas ezManager software via the late-1990s-technology of an infrared syncing cable is a trial in infinite patience; this is not to mention that despite supposed compatibility, I have not ever succeeded in getting the Animas ezManager software to sync with the pump successfully on my iMac running OS X 10.7 Lion); many keep the data they produce locked away in proprietary formats or only offer exporting of data in formats inconvenient or downright useless (e.g., XML) for the average user.

In recent years since the introduction of the iPhone and the rising to dominance of the smart phone paradigm in the cell phone marketplace, a whole host of "logbook" applications have been developed ostensibly to make the management of diabetes-related data easier. However, nearly all--if not all (I speak from the position of having casually but not thoroughly surveyed the landscape)--of these applications continue to require a PWD to manually enter the data s/he wants to track. Given the fact that except on the most very basic of blood glucose meters, every test performed on a blood glucose meter is stored in memory, this strikes me as a completely unnecessary replication of effort. The act of performing a blood glucose test is itself an act of record-making; to require that the PWD take the extra step of entering the result of this test into a data-tracking application is silly and wasteful. The same goes for insulin doses, at least where insulin pumps are involved.

My Solution

All of this is an introduction to the tools I'm developing in this repository. The tools here all rely on the export formats that are available from various diabetes devices. (At the moment, of course, this is limited to the devices I personally use.) My goal is to create software tools that will be truly cross-platform, freely available (and open source), and easy to use. The goal of these tools is to integrate data regarding all aspects of life that affect diabetes (including meals, carbohydrate counts, exercise, sleep, etc., as well as blood glucose readings and insulin doses) and present that data in engaging, informative, and in appropriate cases interactive visualizations.

If you would like to be involved in testing these software tools, you may contact me via e-mail. Be aware that in their present state, the tools I've developed are not yet user-friendly: you need to be familiar with the command line in order to use these tools.

The latest version of (some of) the output from these tools is visible here. What's shown is the following:

  • six days of data from my Dexcom continuous glucose monitor

    • This is the gray line with shading underneath.
    • Moving your mouse over the graph shows you the readings at each point.

  • blood glucose measurements from my blood glucose meter

    • These are the green dots.

  • records of my carbohydrate intake

    • These are the red overlays.
    • You can click on these to see the carbohydrate count in grams along with a description of what I ate and my assessment of the confidence I have in the carbohydrate count.

The logic behind the width of the red overlays is that it takes about 90-120 minutes (90 minutes precisely is what's shown in the graph) for blood glucose to peak after ingesting carbohydrates.

About

The software tools in this repository are provided as aids for extracting data from files exported from the software that comes with certain diabetes-related medical devices (namely, continuous glucose monitors and insulin pumps) and visualizing that data. None of the tools in this repository is intended to substitute for professional medical advice regarding your diabetes care. Consult with your health care provider before making any treatment decisions or changes.
